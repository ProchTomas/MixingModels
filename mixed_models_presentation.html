Recursive Estimation of ARX Model with Parameters Dependent on High-Dimensional Discrete-Valued Regressors

Miroslav Kárný, Tomáš Procházka

Department of Adaptive Systems, Institute of Information Theory and Automation, Czech Academy of Sciences

The Problem

Motivation: Physical Machine Settings

  • Materials science experiments involve expensive, time-consuming physical runs.
  • The Problem: Very low amounts of data relative to the configuration space.
  • Standard regression models and NNs overfit and/or fail to capture localized variances.
  • Quite common problem of learning with small amounts of data.

Problem Definition: g, z, y

Input Features

Settings Vector g: long and categorical

e.g., [frequency, substrate, operator, ...]

Regressors z: short and numerical

Physical continuous inputs (initial and working pressure).

Output Target

Response y: numerical (scalar)

Observed physical outcome of the setting (thickness).

Learning of p(y | z, g, Θ) needs too much data

Approach: Pooled Sub-Models

Instead of one complex model, we build a Pool of 'Experts':

Sub-Model Unit

  • For each category j, in each entry i of g: g_ij.
  • Simple 1D Linear ARX Model.
  • Initiated with non-informative priors.

Adaptive Learning

  • Updates via recursive Bayesian estimation.
  • Employs Optimal Forgetting.
  • Adapts to the system automatically.
  • Calculates sub-model's sufficient statistics.

The Combination Logic

Single predictive distribution is a weighted geometric mean of individual predictors:

p ( y | z , g , data ) ∝ ∏ i,j p ( y | z , g i j , data ) ϕ ij

Combining sub-models into a unified forecast by minimizing relative-entropy:

F ( ϕ ) = KLD ( ϕ ∥ ϕ 0 ) − ∫ ∏ i,j p ( y | z , g i j , data ) ϕ ij d y

The optimal weights ϕ minimize F(ϕ)

The Learning Goal: Recommendation of settings

Inverse Optimization

Search for the optimal g.

  • Given a target response y*.
  • Exhaustive search over g entries maximizing p(y* | z, g, data).
  • Recommends settings to achieve the physical goal.

Validation: Leave-One-Out (LOO)

Methodology

  • Hold out one observation (y, z, g).
  • Train the combined model on all remaining N-1 points.
  • Predict the held-out point using found model.
  • Repeat for all N observations.

Benchmarked against two standard references:

  • Mean Baseline: Average of N-1 observations.
  • Simple OLS: Ordinary Least Squares on continuous z only.

Future Work & Conclusions

  • Work in Progress: Refining the recursive estimation of the ARX parameters and testing robustness on larger categorical dimensions.
  • Hierarchical Priors: Worth exploring to share information across categories with physical similarities, further mitigating data scarcity.
  • A Universal Challenge: This is a hard and common problem across multiple high-stakes industries where data collection is expensive:
    • Semiconductors: Photolithography settings and wafer variability.
    • Aerospace: Turbine blade alloy composition and additive manufacturing.
    • Pharmaceuticals: Batch process optimization and bioreactor control.
    • Precision Engineering: CNC machining tolerances with variable tool wear.
